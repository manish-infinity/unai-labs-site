## Lesson 1.1 — Your brain is an organ, and you can change its health (lesson TBD)

<p><em>Reading time: about 6 minutes</em></p>
<p>Here is a strange fact about modern life: most people take better care of their phone than their brain. The phone gets charged every night, kept updated, protected in a case. The brain that runs the whole show gets four hours of sleep, a skipped lunch, and a day of low-grade stress — and then we wonder why focus and memory feel worse than they used to. This first lesson resets that. Before we talk about any habit, you need one idea firmly in place: your brain is a physical organ, its performance depends on how you treat it, and that means its health is something you can actually change.</p>
<h2>A hungry, living organ</h2>
<p>Your brain is roughly 2% of your body weight but uses about 20% of your energy. It never switches off — even in deep sleep it is busy. To do its work it needs a constant supply of oxygen and glucose delivered by blood, which is why anything that helps your heart and blood vessels tends to help your brain, and anything that harms them harms your brain too. This is the first reason the "body" and the "brain" cannot be treated separately: the brain is an organ fed by the same circulation as the rest of you.</p>
<h2>Neurons, connections, and the cells that keep them alive</h2>
<p>The brain runs on roughly 86 billion neurons, but the number that matters more is the connections between them — the synapses, which number in the trillions and change throughout your life. Learning, remembering, and skill all live in the pattern and strength of those connections. Supporting the neurons is a second cast of cells, the glia, that do maintenance work: feeding neurons, insulating their wiring, clearing waste, and managing inflammation. When people say "brain health," what they usually mean, underneath the marketing, is keeping this whole system — neurons, connections, blood supply, and maintenance crew — in good working order.</p>
<h2>Health is a set of daily inputs, not a fixed trait</h2>
<p>It is tempting to think of your mental sharpness as a fixed number you were born with. It isn't. Within the limits of genetics and age, your brain's day-to-day function rises and falls with its operating conditions: how well you slept, whether you moved, what you ate, how much stress you're carrying, whether you feel connected to other people. Change the inputs and the output changes — sometimes within a single day. That is the whole premise of this course, and it's genuinely good news: the levers are ordinary, and most of them are free.</p>
<h2>What this course will and won't do</h2>
<p>This course teaches evidence-based habits for a healthier, sharper brain. It is not medical advice, and it can't diagnose or treat any condition — if something feels seriously wrong with your memory, mood, or thinking, that's a conversation for your doctor, not a course. What it will do is give you a clear, honest picture of the small number of things that actually move the needle, and a realistic plan to build them into an ordinary week.</p>
<h2>Key takeaways</h2>
<ul>
<li>The brain is a physical, energy-hungry organ fed by your bloodstream — what's good for your heart is generally good for your brain.</li>
<li>Brain function lives in the trillions of changeable connections between neurons, maintained by glial "support" cells.</li>
<li>Your mental sharpness is not a fixed trait; it rises and falls with daily inputs you control.</li>
<li>Brain health is a set of habits, not a supplement or a one-time fix — and most of the highest-leverage habits are free.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Try this</h2>
<p>For the next 24 hours, notice one link between how you treated your body and how your mind performed: a foggy afternoon after a short night, a clearer head after a walk, a scattered feeling after skipping a meal. Write down the one you noticed. You're training yourself to see the brain as an organ that responds to its conditions — the mindset the rest of this course builds on.</p>

## Lesson 1.2 — The five levers of brain health (lesson TBD)

<p><em>Reading time: about 7 minutes</em></p>
<p>If your brain's performance depends on its daily inputs, the obvious question is: which inputs? You could spend a lifetime chasing every study and supplement. You don't need to. When you strip brain-health research down to what's robust and repeatable, the same short list keeps appearing. This lesson introduces the five levers this whole course is organized around — sleep, movement, nutrition, stress, and connection — and, just as importantly, how they work together.</p>
<h2>The five levers</h2>
<p><strong>Sleep</strong> is the foundation: it's when your brain clears waste and consolidates memory, and it's the lever that most directly wrecks the others when it's missing. <strong>Movement</strong> increases blood flow and triggers the release of growth factors that help the brain form and keep connections. <strong>Nutrition</strong> supplies the raw materials and steady fuel your neurons run on. <strong>Stress</strong>, in chronic form, is the main lever working <em>against</em> you — useful in short bursts, corrosive when it never switches off. And <strong>connection</strong> — real relationships and a sense of purpose — turns out to be a genuine cognitive protector, not a soft extra. Modules 2 through 4 take these in turn.</p>
<h2>They work as a system, not a checklist</h2>
<p>The reason this short list is so powerful is that the levers amplify each other. A good night's sleep makes it easier to exercise and to resist stress-eating; exercise deepens your sleep and buffers stress; managing stress protects your sleep and your appetite; connection lowers stress and gives you reasons to keep the other habits. The flip side is also true — pull one lever down and it drags the others with it. Miss sleep and your food choices get worse, your stress tolerance drops, and your motivation to move evaporates. This is why "fix everything at once" fails and why picking your weakest lever first, as we'll do, works so well.</p>
<h2>The 80/20 of brain health</h2>
<p>Almost everyone wants to skip to the interesting-sounding optimizations — the supplement stack, the cold plunge, the brain-training app. But if the five fundamentals are shaky, none of that matters. Getting these five roughly right is the 80/20: it delivers most of the available benefit for most people, and it does so with things you're already doing every day, just done more deliberately. Optimization is what you earn <em>after</em> the fundamentals are in place, not a substitute for them.</p>
<h2>Where the levers show up next</h2>
<p>The rest of the course is these five levers, in depth and in practice. Module 2 is sleep. Module 3 is movement and nutrition — the body-brain connection. Module 4 is stress, mood, connection, and the long game of cognitive longevity. Along the way, each module points you to a matching brain-health article on the site if you want to go deeper on one lever. By the end, you'll assemble the best habit from each into a single weekly plan.</p>
<h2>Key takeaways</h2>
<ul>
<li>Five levers do most of the work in brain health: sleep, movement, nutrition, stress, and connection.</li>
<li>They function as an interconnected system — each one strengthens or undermines the others.</li>
<li>Getting the five fundamentals roughly right is the 80/20; optimizations come after, not instead.</li>
<li>Your weakest lever is your highest-leverage target — improving it lifts the whole system.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Try this</h2>
<p>Rate yourself right now, 1 to 5, on each lever: sleep, movement, nutrition, stress, connection. Don't overthink it — go with your gut. Circle the lowest score. That lever is where this course will pay off fastest for you, and it's the one to keep in mind as you work through the modules ahead. (This is also your Week 1 exercise — keep the numbers.)</p>

## Lesson 1.3 — Myths, marketing, and what actually works (lesson TBD)

<p><em>Reading time: about 7 minutes</em></p>
<p>Brain health is a big market, and big markets attract big claims. Before you build any habit, it helps to know which popular ideas are wrong, which products are mostly hope in a bottle, and how to read a brain-health claim without getting fooled. Clearing this ground now means you'll spend your effort where it actually pays off — on the fundamentals from the last lesson — instead of on expensive shortcuts.</p>
<h2>The myth that won't die: "you only use 10% of your brain"</h2>
<p>You use all of it. Brain imaging shows activity across the entire brain over the course of a day, and different regions doing different jobs; there is no dormant 90% waiting to be unlocked. The myth is appealing because it promises hidden potential, which is exactly why so many products lean on it. When a claim implies there's a secret reserve of brainpower a gadget or supplement can switch on, that's your signal to be skeptical, not excited.</p>
<h2>Brain-training games and supplements: what the evidence really shows</h2>
<p>Brain-training apps reliably make you better at one thing: the game itself. The evidence that those gains "transfer" to real-world memory, focus, or intelligence is weak — large reviews have found little support for broad transfer. Playing them isn't harmful, but it isn't the shortcut it's sold as. Supplements and "nootropics" are a similar story: for people who eat a reasonable diet, most over-the-counter brain supplements have thin or mixed evidence, aren't tightly regulated, and can interact with medications. The uncomfortable truth the marketing hides is that sleep, movement, food, stress management, and connection beat almost anything you can buy in a bottle.</p>
<h2>How to read a brain-health claim</h2>
<p>You don't need a science degree to filter the noise. A few questions do most of the work. Is this correlation being sold as cause — did the thing actually make people sharper, or were sharper people just more likely to do it already? How many people were studied, and for how long — a glowing result from twelve participants over two weeks proves very little. Who benefits from you believing it — is there a product attached? And does the claim sound too clean — one weird trick, a guaranteed fix, a number that seems suspiciously precise? Real brain health is unglamorous and gradual, which is precisely why it doesn't make for exciting ads.</p>
<h2>The honest bottom line</h2>
<p>The things that genuinely protect and sharpen your brain are the boring fundamentals: sleep well, move your body, eat reasonably, manage chronic stress, stay connected, and keep learning. They're unglamorous, they're mostly free, and they work. Everything in the rest of this course is a practical, specific way to do those things — no secret reserve to unlock, no bottle to buy. That's not a limitation; it's the reason it actually works.</p>
<h2>Key takeaways</h2>
<ul>
<li>The "10% of your brain" idea is a myth — you use all of it; treat any claim that promises hidden brainpower with suspicion.</li>
<li>Brain-training games mostly make you better at the game; broad transfer to real-life cognition has weak evidence.</li>
<li>For people eating a reasonable diet, most brain supplements have thin, mixed, or poorly regulated evidence.</li>
<li>Filter claims by asking: correlation or cause, how many people and how long, who profits, and does it sound too clean?</li>
<li>The fundamentals — sleep, movement, nutrition, stress, connection, learning — beat almost anything you can buy.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Try this</h2>
<p>Think of one brain-health product or claim you've seen recently — an app, a supplement, a headline. Run it through the four questions: correlation or cause, sample size and length, who profits, too clean? Notice how quickly most claims wobble. That filter is now yours to keep, and it will save you real money and time.</p>
